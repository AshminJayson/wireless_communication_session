5G / Definition

What is 5G?

The fifth generation of mobile networks — designed as an intelligent, connected ecosystem for people, machines, and industries, not just faster handsets.

Core objectives
  • Extreme speed and capacity.
  • Ultra-low latency for real-time control.
  • Massive device connectivity.
  • Reliability for mission-critical services.
20 Gbps
Peak data-rate target · ITU IMT-2020
~1 ms

Stress 5G is a platform. The three numbers are ITU IMT-2020 targets — ideal, not what a handset shows today. Set that expectation honestly.

5G / Core Technology — The Air Interface

Massive MIMO & beamforming

Massive MIMO

Multiple-Input, Multiple-Output.

  • Dozens to hundreds of antennas per cell.
  • Serves many users at once.
  • Far more capacity and coverage.

Beamforming

Focused, steerable signals.

  • Aims energy at each specific user.
  • Less waste in empty directions.
  • Better signal strength and efficiency.

Floodlight vs. steerable spotlight — and squarely the hardware/RF side of the system.

MIMO = more antennas, more parallel streams. Beamforming = aiming instead of broadcasting. Floodlight vs. spotlight. This is my world as a hardware engineer — speak to it.
5G / Architecture & Standards

An architecture defined by 3GPP

3GPP — the 3rd Generation Partnership Project — is the global body that standardizes mobile networks. It specifies how the UE, RAN, and 5G Core interwork, published as numbered Releases.

3GPP is the standards body behind every mobile generation. Make the point that 5G isn't one vendor's design - it's a shared specification, released in numbered Releases (Rel-15 was the first full 5G in 2018; Rel-16/17/18 add "5G-Advanced"). The labels on the arrows (Uu, N2/N3, N6) are the actual 3GPP reference points. NSA reused the 4G core first; SA is the full 5G core. Walk left to right.
5G / The Three Domains

UE, RAN & 5G Core

User Equipment

UE

The device, its 5G modem, and the (U)SIM that identifies it on the network.

  • Phones, vehicles, cellular IoT modules.
  • Modem + (U)SIM identity.
  • Speaks NR over the air (Uu).

Radio Access Network

NG-RAN

The radio layer — gNB base stations linking devices to the core.

  • gNB — the 5G base station.
  • Massive MIMO & beamforming.
  • Split into CU / DU / RU.

5G Core

5GC

A cloud-native, service-based core that authenticates, routes, and manages sessions.

  • Service-based architecture.
  • Functions: AMF / SMF / UPF.
  • Control-plane / user-plane split.

Control / user-plane separation and the service-based core are what make slicing and edge breakout possible.

Three domains. UE is my world as a hardware engineer - the device, modem, and SIM; at Cavli we build cellular IoT modules, which are exactly UE. RAN is the radio: gNB with Massive MIMO, now split into CU/DU/RU for flexible deployment. The 5G Core is cloud-native and service-based - AMF handles access and mobility, SMF handles sessions, UPF moves user traffic. The control/user-plane split is what enables network slicing and local edge breakout (the topics from the old slides live here now).

The Road to 6G

Still research today — but the direction is set: faster, near-instant, and intelligence-native.

Looking ahead. 6G is research today, but the direction is clear — and where a lot of work points now.
6G / The Next Leap

Why 6G, and what to expect

Why we need it
  • Today's networks cap out for the most demanding future apps.
  • Relentless growth in data and connectivity demand.
  • Immersive, AI-driven experiences 5G can't fully serve.
Expected features
  • Speeds up to ~1 Tbps (research target).
  • Near-zero latency — sub-millisecond.
  • AI-native — intelligence built in, not bolted on.
  • Enhanced reliability, end to end.
6G isn't just faster — it's AI-native with near-zero latency. Frame ~1 Tbps as an aspirational research target, not a committed spec, to keep the numbers credible.

What makes 6G possible

AIIntelligence in the network
